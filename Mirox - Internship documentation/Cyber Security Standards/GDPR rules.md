## Chapter 1 — General Provisions

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**1**|Subject Matter & Objectives|Protects people’s personal data and privacy while allowing data to move freely between EU countries.|
|**2**|Material Scope|Applies to all activities where personal data is collected or used for work or business purposes; doesn’t cover personal or national-security use.|
|**3**|Territorial Scope|Applies to any company inside the EU and to companies outside the EU that offer services to or track people in the EU.|
|**4**|Definitions|Explains key terms like personal data, processing, controller, processor, and data subject used throughout GDPR.|

## **Chapter 2 — Principles**

---

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**5**|Principles Relating to Processing of Personal Data|Lists the six main rules for handling personal data — be fair and transparent, collect only what's needed, keep it accurate, store it only as long as necessary, and keep it secure.|
|**6**|Lawfulness of Processing|Says data can be used only when there's a valid reason such as consent, a contract, a legal duty, protection of life, public interest, or a company's legitimate interest.|
|**7**|Conditions for Consent|Explains that consent must be clear, freely given, and easy to withdraw; no pre-ticked boxes or hidden permissions allowed.|
|**8**|Conditions Applicable to Children's Consent|Children under 16 (or 13 in some countries) need permission from a parent or guardian before their data is processed.|
|**9**|Processing of Special Categories of Personal Data|Sensitive data such as health, religion, or ethnicity can be used only in special situations with strong protection or explicit consent.|
|**10**|Processing of Personal Data Relating to Criminal Convictions and Offences|Information about crimes or offences can be handled only by authorities or if allowed by law.|
|**11**|Processing Not Requiring Identification|If you don't need to know who someone is, you don't have to identify them just to follow GDPR — anonymous data stays outside most rules.|

## **Chapter 3 — Rights of Individuals**

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**12**|Transparent Information, Communication and Modalities|People must be clearly told how their data is used, in simple and easy-to-understand language.|
|**13**|Information to be Provided When Data is Collected from the Person|When collecting data directly from someone, tell them what data you take, why you need it, how long you keep it, and who gets it.|
|**14**|Information to be Provided When Data is Not Obtained from the Person|If data comes from another source, the person still has to be informed about it and how it's used.|
|**15**|Right of Access|People can ask to see all personal data a company holds about them and get a copy of it.|
|**16**|Right to Rectification|People can have incorrect or outdated personal data corrected quickly.|
|**17**|Right to Erasure (Right to be Forgotten)|People can ask for their personal data to be deleted when it's no longer needed or was used unlawfully.|
|**18**|Right to Restrict Processing|People can ask a company to temporarily stop using their data while accuracy or legal issues are checked.|
|**19**|Notification Obligation|If a company changes, deletes, or limits someone's data, it must tell all others who received that data.|
|**20**|Right to Data Portability|People can ask for their data in a usable format so they can move it to another service provider.|
|**21**|Right to Object|People can object to their data being used, especially for marketing or profiling, and the company must stop unless it has strong reasons to continue.|
|**22**|Automated Individual Decision-Making, Including Profiling|People have the right not to be judged only by automated systems (like algorithms) without human involvement.|
|**23**|Restrictions|Governments can limit some of these rights for reasons such as national security or criminal investigations.|

## **Chapter 4 — Controller and Processor Duties**

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**24**|Responsibility of the Controller|The company that decides how data is used (the controller) must make sure data is processed safely and lawfully.|
|**25**|Data Protection by Design and by Default|Privacy must be built into systems and products from the start and default settings should protect data automatically.|
|**26**|Joint Controllers|When two or more organizations share control of data, they must clearly agree on who is responsible for what.|
|**27**|Representatives of Non-EU Controllers or Processors|Companies outside the EU that handle EU data must appoint an EU-based representative to communicate with authorities.|
|**28**|Processor|A processor (who handles data for another company) must follow written instructions and protect the data properly.|
|**29**|Processing Under the Authority of the Controller or Processor|Employees or contractors can use data only under the clear instructions of their company.|
|**30**|Records of Processing Activities|Every company must keep written records of what personal data they collect, why, and how it's stored or shared.|
|**31**|Cooperation with Supervisory Authority|Organizations must cooperate with data protection regulators when asked for information or during investigations.|
|**32**|Security of Processing|Personal data must be protected with technical and organizational security measures such as encryption and access control.|
|**33**|Notification of a Personal Data Breach to the Authority|If data is leaked or exposed, the company must report it to the data protection authority within 72 hours.|
|**34**|Communication of a Personal Data Breach to the Data Subject|If the breach could harm people, the company must also inform the affected individuals.|
|**35**|Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)|Before starting high-risk projects, companies must check for privacy risks and document how they'll reduce them.|
|**36**|Prior Consultation|If a DPIA shows very high risk, the company must consult the data protection authority before continuing.|
|**37**|Designation of the Data Protection Officer (DPO)|Organizations that handle large amounts of sensitive data must appoint a DPO to oversee data protection.|
|**38**|Position of the Data Protection Officer|The DPO must work independently and report directly to senior management.|
|**39**|Tasks of the Data Protection Officer|The DPO advises, trains staff, monitors compliance, and cooperates with regulators.|
|**40**|Codes of Conduct|Industry groups can create privacy guidelines to help members apply GDPR correctly.|
|**41**|Monitoring of Approved Codes of Conduct|Independent bodies can be approved to monitor compliance with these codes.|
|**42**|Certification|Companies can voluntarily apply for GDPR certification to prove good privacy practices.|
|**43**|Certification Bodies|Only accredited organizations can issue GDPR certificates after checking compliance.|

## Chapter 5 — Transfers of Personal Data to Third Countries or International Organisations

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**44**|General Principle for Transfers|Personal data can be sent outside the EU only if the same level of protection travels with it.|
|**45**|Transfers on the Basis of an Adequacy Decision|Data may go to countries the EU officially says have strong privacy laws (like Japan or Switzerland).|
|**46**|Transfers Subject to Appropriate Safeguards|If no adequacy decision exists, companies must use safeguards such as contract clauses or corporate rules to protect the data.|
|**47**|Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)|Multinational groups can make internal privacy rules, approved by regulators, to allow safe data sharing within their companies worldwide.|
|**48**|Transfers or Disclosures Not Authorised by EU Law|Non-EU court or government requests cannot override GDPR unless approved under EU or national law.|
|**49**|Derogations for Specific Situations|One-time transfers are allowed in special cases such as explicit consent, contract performance, or public-interest reasons.|
|**50**|International Co-operation for the Protection of Personal Data|The EU encourages global cooperation between privacy authorities to enforce data protection standards everywhere.|

## **Chapter 6 — Supervisory Authorities**

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**51**|Supervisory Authority|Each EU country must have at least one independent authority to monitor how GDPR is followed.|
|**52**|Independence|The authority must act independently and cannot take orders from any government or organization.|
|**53**|General Conditions for Members|The head and members of the authority must be chosen fairly, qualified in privacy law, and protected from unfair removal.|
|**54**|Rules on Establishment|Each EU country defines how its data protection authority operates, including powers, funding, and staffing.|
|**55**|Competence|Each authority oversees GDPR compliance within its own country.|
|**56**|Competence of the Lead Supervisory Authority|If a company operates in several EU countries, one "lead authority" takes charge to coordinate cross-border cases.|
|**57**|Tasks|The authority's main jobs are to handle complaints, monitor compliance, give advice, and raise awareness about data protection.|
|**58**|Powers|The authority can investigate, order companies to fix violations, suspend data flows, and issue fines.|
|**59**|Activity Reports|Every authority must publish an annual report about its work, findings, and enforcement actions.|

## Chapter 7 — Cooperation and Consistency

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**60**|Cooperation Between the Lead Authority and Other Authorities|When a company operates in multiple EU countries, all concerned authorities cooperate, led by one "lead authority."|
|**61**|Mutual Assistance|Authorities help each other by sharing information and supporting investigations across borders.|
|**62**|Joint Operations of Supervisory Authorities|Two or more authorities can carry out joint investigations or inspections together.|
|**63**|Consistency Mechanism|The EU ensures GDPR rules are applied consistently by all authorities, avoiding different interpretations.|
|**64**|Opinion of the European Data Protection Board (EDPB)|The EDPB can issue official opinions to resolve disagreements between authorities.|
|**65**|Dispute Resolution by the EDPB|If authorities disagree on a case, the EDPB makes the final binding decision.|
|**66**|Urgency Procedure|If urgent action is needed to protect people's rights, an authority can act immediately, even before full review.|
|**67**|Exchange of Information|Authorities and the EDPB exchange information securely to improve cooperation.|
|**68**|European Data Protection Board (EDPB)|The EDPB is the central EU body that guides all national authorities and ensures uniform GDPR enforcement.|
|**69**|Independence of the EDPB|The EDPB acts independently, without influence from governments or companies.|
|**70**|Tasks of the EDPB|The EDPB advises the EU, issues guidelines, settles disputes, and promotes consistent GDPR application.|
|**71**|Reports|The EDPB must publish annual reports about its activities, opinions, and decisions.|
|**72**|Procedure|Describes how the EDPB conducts its meetings and adopts decisions.|
|**73**|Chair|The EDPB elects a chair and two deputy chairs to manage its work.|
|**74**|Secretariat|The EDPB's secretariat, provided by the European Data Protection Supervisor, supports its day-to-day operations.|
|**75**|Confidentiality|Members of the EDPB and authorities must keep case details confidential.|
|**76**|EU Relations and Transparency|The EDPB cooperates with EU institutions and maintains transparency while protecting sensitive information.|

## Chapter 8 — Remedies, Liability, and Penalties

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**77**|Right to Lodge a Complaint with a Supervisory Authority|Anyone can file a complaint with their country's data protection authority if their data rights are violated.|
|**78**|Right to an Effective Judicial Remedy Against a Supervisory Authority|People can go to court if they disagree with how a data authority handled their complaint.|
|**79**|Right to an Effective Judicial Remedy Against a Controller or Processor|Individuals can take a company or processor to court if their data is misused or handled unlawfully.|
|**80**|Representation of Data Subjects|People can choose an organization (like a privacy NGO) to represent them in complaints or lawsuits.|
|**81**|Suspension of Proceedings|Courts can pause ongoing cases if the same issue is already being reviewed by another EU court.|
|**82**|Right to Compensation and Liability|Anyone harmed by GDPR violations can claim financial compensation from the company or processor responsible.|
|**83**|General Conditions for Imposing Administrative Fines|Authorities can fine organizations up to €20 million or 4% of annual global turnover for serious GDPR breaches.|
|**84**|Penalties|EU countries can set additional penalties (besides fines) for breaking GDPR rules, such as warnings or temporary data bans.|

## Chapter 9 — Provisions Relating to Specific Data Processing Situations

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**85**|Processing and Freedom of Expression and Information|GDPR must balance privacy with freedom of speech — for example, journalists can publish stories using data if it's in the public interest.|
|**86**|Processing and Public Access to Official Documents|Public authorities can share official documents if allowed by law, even if they contain personal data.|
|**87**|Processing of National Identification Numbers|Countries can make their own laws for using national ID numbers, as long as privacy is protected.|
|**88**|Processing in the Context of Employment|Employers can process employee data (like attendance or salary) only for legitimate work-related purposes, with privacy safeguards.|
|**89**|Safeguards and Derogations for Research and Statistics|Data can be used for research or statistics if proper protections (like anonymization) are in place to protect individuals.|
|**90**|Obligations of Secrecy|Professionals with confidentiality duties (like doctors or lawyers) must still follow GDPR while protecting client or patient secrets.|
|**91**|Existing Data Protection Rules of Churches and Religious Associations|Churches or religious groups can keep their own privacy rules if they align with GDPR standards.|

## Chapter 10 — Delegated Acts and Implementing Acts

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**92**|Exercise of the Delegation|The European Commission can make detailed rules or updates to GDPR (called "delegated acts") to keep it up to date with new technologies or needs.|
|**93**|Committee Procedure|A special committee helps the European Commission review and approve these delegated or implementing acts before they become official.|

## **Chapter 11 — Final Provisions**

|**Article**|**Title**|**Rules**|
|---|---|---|
|**94**|Repeal of Previous Directive|GDPR replaces the old 1995 EU Data Protection Directive (Directive 95/46/EC).|
|**95**|Relationship with e-Privacy Directive|GDPR works together with the e-Privacy Directive, which covers cookies, emails, and online communications.|
|**96**|Relationship with Existing Agreements|GDPR doesn't cancel older international data agreements but ensures they meet GDPR's protection standards.|
|**97**|Commission Reports|The European Commission must review and report how GDPR is working every four years.|
|**98**|Review of Other EU Laws|Other EU laws related to data protection must be updated to stay consistent with GDPR.|
|**99**|Entry into Force and Application|GDPR officially became law on May 25, 2018, and has been enforceable since that date.|
